Nomenclature Rules Revisions - October 1998

The International Committee for Standardised Genetic Nomenclature for Mice meeting in Garmisch-Partenkirchen in October 1998.

Revised rules - Gene Nomenclature

1.3 Rules for Naming Transgenes

1B Insert designation

amend 3rd sentence to read "Italics, super- or subscripts, internal spaces, and punctuation should not be used, except hyphens used to separate two parts of a compound transgene as described below".

Section a:

Delete "Hyphens are omitted when using hyphenated gene symbols" and replace with "If the insertion is a compound construct consisting of the promoter of one gene linked to the coding region of another, the insertion may be designated using the symbol of the first, separated by a hyphen from the symbol of the second. One or both symbols should be truncated if the total number of letters exceeds six or the insert designation and the laboratory assigned number (C. a. below) together are 11 characters or less. Different promoter/gene constructs can be distinguished by the different serial numbers and lab codes added as in C below".

3.3 Coisogenic, congenic and segregating inbred strains

2. Congenic strains

After first sentence, delete rest of paragraph and replace with: "They should be designated by a compound symbol consisting of three parts. The full or abbreviated symbol of the donor strain is separated by a period from an abbreviated symbol of the donor strain. (In cases where the donor is not inbred or is complex, the symbol Cg can be used to denote congenic. The use of the donor strain symbol or Cg is essential to distinguish congenic from coisogenic strains). A hyphen then separates the strains from the differential locus or loci introgressed from the donor strain (in italics) e.g. B10.129-H12b is a strain with the genetic background og C57BL/10Sn (=B10) but which differs from that strain by the introduction of a differential allele (H12b) derived from strain 129/J. If several lines derived from the same donor strain are available, the individual lines may be distinguished by a number and/or a letter in parentheses e.g. B10.129(12M)-H12b, B10.129(21M)-H4b. Lab codes and serial numbers may also be used (see below). When the chromosomal segment that has been transferred is defined by several genes or DNA loci, the segment can be defined in the symbol by listing the most proximal and the most distal markers demonstrated to be in the segment in parentheses, separated by a hyphen. e.g. B6.Cg-D4Mit25-D4Mit80 is a congenic strain made by introducing into C57BL/6J a segment of Chromosome 4 from an outbred or mixed strain, extending between the two defined markers. B6.CBA-D4Mit25-D4Mit80 is a similar congenic strain in which the donor chromosomal segment comes from the CBA/J strain. Note that the markers defining the segment only describe the most proximal and distal markers tested, and this does not imply that there are not other untested markers further proximal or distal. If several lines are made, in the same or different labs, that have the same segment and would be otherwise indistinguishable, then a serial number and lab code should be appended. Congenic lines that differ at a histocompatibility locus and therefore resist each other's grafts are called congenic resistant (CR) lines".

At the end of the second paragraph add:

"Marker assisted breeding or marker assisted selection breeding, also known as "speed congenics"permits the production of congenic strains equivalent to 10 backcross generations in as few as 5 generations. (Markel et al (1997) Nature Genetics 17. 280-284; Wakeland et al (1997) Immunology Today 18. 472-477). Provided that the appropriate marker selection has been used, these are termed congenic strains if the donor strain contribution unlinked to selected locus or chromosomal region is less than 0.1% (i.e. NE10)".
