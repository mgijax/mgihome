MGI User Survey Results

In the Spring of 1999, we asked MGI users to complete an MGI User Survey. Many thanks to the nearly 300 researchers who responded. The purpose of this document is to report on the results of the survey. If you did not get a chance to complete a survey, please feel free to give us your comments and suggestions any time.

Suggestions and Feedback

Answers to the short questions, summarized in the preceding section, suggest that MGI users are, by and large, satisfied with MGI resources. We also asked researchers for feedback and suggestions and, indeed, many respondents offered very positive comments including: "Keep up the good work!"; "I find that the system is great for my needs and am not clever enough to think of more needs until you provide them for me"; "Just keep going!"; "I like the oxford grid, and use it as a teaching tools"; "Very user friendly". We greatly appreciate these and all votes of confidence we received from our user community!

One of the goals of our survey was to encourage feedback on changes and improvements that MGI users would like to see in the future. In this section, we summarize these comments.

Regarding user-friendliness of the query forms

About half of 29 respondents find the forms difficult to use in some respects. Some find it hard to know which form to use; others find it hard to figure out how to use particular fields in a form. Apparently, people are not seeing the help documentation. About half of respondents want more definitions of fields, explanations, or examples.

Types of searches that users would like to perform but cannot because the query forms do not provide appropriate options or the database does not seem to contain the data they are interested in.

10 of the 29 respondents were interested in various mapping questions. Several wanted a pathway to connect linkage and physical maps. Others wanted connections between linkage maps and sequence information; or, an easier way to define map position for a marker based on strain distribution pattern profiles for strains; or a way to download physical maps.

A number of respondents are interested in different ways to search on phenotypes and mutations. One suggestion is to provide a checkbox on the OMIM/MLC search form that enables you to choose whether to search both OMIM/MLC or just MLC. Others want ways to search for knock-outs and targeted mutations in MGD. One respondent would like to search for phenotypes where the gene is not known.

There was quite a range of requests and suggestions. Researchers are interested in sequence information; more flexibility in searching for expression information; more options for generating reports as opposed to web tables; easier access to information on mouse strains and where mouse strains can be obtained; more detailed homology information, and, information on non-mammalian species.

Improvements in the MGI web site that users would like to see

There were 86 respondents. Of those, 10 suggested improvements in the user interface (query forms, help documentation). Some examples: facilitate access to search forms and reduce the number of clicks required to navigate from home-page to search to results summary to details; provide a mechanism to search for MIT micro-satellite sequences; a simple search such that you enter a term on a single field and search the whole database (similar to the option in Entrez to search ALL FIELDS for a specified value). Several users would like to see forms and displays that are easier and quicker to work with on a small computer; others want more explanations and definitions especially for users who may not be geneticists. Make it easier to view and print comparative maps.

With regard to data content and presentation of data, users offered a number of suggestions, including: more integration of data, especially physical mapping, RH mapping, and genome sequencing; improved integration of and links between data (e.g., DNA sequence + map position + expression profile + protein structure + biochemical pathway + mutations + phenotypes); improved access to information on transgenic and targeted mutant mice; more options for formatting query results and downloading files; more access to sequence data, more information on inbred strains and more integration with other sources of mouse and rat data.
