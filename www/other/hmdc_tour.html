The Human-Mouse: Disease Connection

The goal of the Human-Mouse: Disease Connection (HMDC) is to provide seamless human-to-mouse data traversal, enabling clinical and translational researchers to take advantage of wealth of data and annotations from mouse models; as well as allowing mouse researchers to connect their findings directly to genetic associations reported in human disease.

The mouse is genetically and physiologically similar to humans, is tractable as a laboratory animal, has a fully sequenced and well-annotated genome, and a readily available set of powerful molecular technologies for manipulating its genome in very precise ways. Now, clinical researchers whose primary focus is on human genetic disease, variants, and natural mutations have a highly-accessible way to explore experimentally characterized mouse mutants for a spectrum of associated phenotypes, as well as known disease models developed by the greater research community. Investigators can begin with genes (symbols, names or IDs) or gene lists (including .vcf files), genome positions (in human or mouse), OMIM diseases or phenotypes, and retrieve a genes, annotated mammalian phenotypes, associated human diseases and/or available mouse models for further research, along with comprehensive supporting references. If you are new to working with mouse models, or encounter terms which are unfamiliar to you, we also encourage you to browse the Introduction to Mouse Genetics in order to aid with the interpretation of your results.

What can you do with the HMDC?

This tool can be used to:

  • use information about known mouse phenotypes to prioritize candidate genes from a list generated by whole genome or exome sequencing
  • use information about known mouse phenotypes to prioritize candidates within a region (QTLs, GWAS, association or family based studies)
  • identify mouse genes which exhibit a phenotype of interest
  • identify human and mouse genetic associations to disease
  • find an experimental mouse model to use in the study of a specific human disease

Overview

Entry into the HMDC is designed to be straightforward. On the homepage, three boxes appear where investigators may choose to enter (1) genes - either individually or multiples, (2) a genome location or set of regions and/or (3) disease name(s) or mammalian phenotype(s).

To enter multiple genes or genomic regions, use a new line for each entry or separate with commas. Human and mouse data may both be used, though for positions, you must specify a single organism. Please also ensure that you are using the current genome assembly (build) or the results will be inaccurate. If you need to convert your data, a simple online remapping tool has been provided by NCBI. Gene names and symbols will automatically match official symbols and synonyms in both species. For multiple disease or phenotype terms, use the autocomplete options for the greatest specificity. If you do not select from the autocomplete list, the search will run based on text-matching. Upload of variant call format (.vcf) files is also supported.

For many experimental questions, a single search box will be sufficient, but if two boxes have search terms entered, the results will reflect cases matching on both categories (boolean AND). While this can be an efficient shortcut, if a very precise phenotype or disease annotation is used, some potentially relevant results may be omitted.

Whichever search box is used, the same set of results tables will appear. To follow along with this example, enter trp53 proc apc cdkn2a into the Genes search box.

Along the top, an orange banner remembers your original query and allows you to quickly modify your results by adding, removing or replacing search terms. A second banner below serves as a header for your results, and indicates which type of matching was run. The results themselves are arranged into three tabbed tables for: Traits & Models (shown), Genes, and Diseases.

  1. Traits & Models tab
    • A list of nine genes and one transgene are returned as the rows in a grid. In each case, the gene name, gene symbol or a published synonym of the gene name or symbol matched some part of the search input. Human orthologs are listed in the first column, with mouse orthologs in the second. Note that human genes are in all capital letters, while murine nomenclature is in sentence case.
    • Systems level mammalian phenotypes (mouse) are shown at the to the left of a solid grey bar, while associated human diseases or published disease models that involved mutant alleles of a given gene are shown to the right. Orange filled boxes will only appear in the annotated human diseases and mouse models section.
    • Left side of grid: If a mutant allele of a given gene has an annotation to an affected system, the box at the intersection of the gene and system will be colored in blue. Dark shades indicate many annotations in that category, while lighter shades are fewer. White cells indicate that no phenotypes or observations have been reported for that gene in that system. Systems only appear if at least one gene (genotype) has an annotation. N is used to indicate that the system was examined and found to be normal. In some cases, N! will be used to indicate background sensitivity. If researchers determined that the mouse carrying a particular mutation was overall ‘normal’, a normal phenotype column will be displayed on the far right of the Mammalian Phenotypes section. Keep in mind that not all mutations disrupt gene function and as this table compresses multiple alleles into a single row, both normal and highly affected systems may appear for the same gene.
    • Right side of grid: If mutations in this gene have been reported in human patients (and that data has been collected by OMIM), an orange fill will be used at the intersection of a gene with its associated human disease. If mutant mice have been reported as disease models, a blue fill will be used. In cases where both mouse models and human clinical cases have been used to support involvement of orthologous genes, a two-toned fill will appear. White indicates that the intersection of a given gene and human disease has not been reported.
    • Filtering: Results can be filtered by clicking the boxes that appear in each row and column, adding check marks. If a system, disease or gene symbol match is not relevant to your experimental question, leave them unselected and then click the [Submit Filters] button in the top-left. This will filter all tabs. Filters can be removed by clicking on the [(x) remove filters] note which will appear just below the orange Results header bar.

The goal is to pull together both human and mouse data into a single flexible tool which can be used to answer a variety of biological and especially disease-related questions

This tool can be used to: - use information about known mouse phenotypes to prioritize candidate genes from a list (whole genome sequencing, exome sequencing) - use information about known mouse phenotypes to prioritize candidates within a region (QTLs, GWAS, association or family based studies) - identify mouse genes and genetic which have been annotated to a disease - identify mouse genes which are known to be involved within a specific biological system - find an experimental mouse model to use in the study of a specific human disease
